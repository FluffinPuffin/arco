Recap

  • Written as two stacked numbers at the beginning of a line
  • The top number shows how many beats are in each measure
  • The bottom number tells us what type of note aligns with the beat

Explore how time signatures organize rhythm in music, including how they indicate the number of beats in each measure and which type of note receives one beat.

Hi! Welcome to lesson 2.4. In this lesson we’re talking about time signatures. A time signature is important because it tells us how many beats are in each measure. Which essentially tells us how we count through our song. A time signature is created by placing two numbers on top of each other. This is an example of what a time signature looks like, you see how we have a four over four. The top number is responsible for telling us how many beats are in each measure. Or, what are we counting to? If the top number is a four, we’re counting one, two, three, four. The bottom number is responsible for telling us what type of note is going to match up with the beat. In this case, four just equals quarter note. For example, a time signature of three four, or three quarters, means that we are counting to three and the quarter note is going to line up with the beat. Here is an example of what a four four time signature sounds like. It’s very square and sounds like we’re marching. Ready? One, two, three, four. (playing violin). Here is an example of what a three four time signature sounds like. Three four is called the waltz time signature and it sounds like this. One, two, three, one, two, three. (playing violin). The number on the top of a time signature can range from two to even twelve while the bottom number matches up with our notes so we can have a four on the bottom for quarter note, a two on the bottom for half note, or an eight on the bottom for an eighth note. So you could have a time signature of twelve eight, or six eight or even five four, this just tells us how many beats to count and what type of note is going to match the beat.
