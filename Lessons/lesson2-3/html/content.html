Recap

  • Every note has a silent partner called a "rest"
  • When we rest in music, we pause and stay silent
  • Four basic rests: Whole, half, quarter, and eighth
  • Rests tell us how long to be silent

Learn about the four basic types of rests in music: whole rest, half rest, quarter rest, and eighth rest.

Hi! Welcome to lesson 2.3 where we talk about types of rests. You might be wondering, what is a rest? A rest is a silent partner to a note. Where a note creates sound, a rest means silence. We are going to focus on four types of rests for this course. The first one, you realize it matches the whole note, this is a whole rest. A little box hanging from the fourth line. When we see this, we are silent for four beats. Just, one, two, three, four (clapping), no sound. The next type of rest is called a half rest. Just like the half note, the half rest is worth two beats of silence. When we play we just rest one, two (clapping), no sound. The next type of rest is called a quarter rest, just like the quarter note, it is worth one beat of silence. The last type of rest is called an eighth rest, just like the eighth note, this is worth half of a beat of silence. It’s a very quick rest. I want to show you what this looks like in action. If I had a piece of music that showed a half rest, a half note, and a whole note this is what it would sound like. The first two beats would be silent, the next two would be a half note, an A, and the next whole note would be a whole note on E. That would sound like, one, two. Three, four, one, two, three, four (while playing). You might have noticed that the whole rest and the half rest look fairly similar. Here is a trick to tell them apart. The half rest sits on top of the line and looks more like a hat. The whole rest sits underneath the line almost like it’s a hole in the ground.
